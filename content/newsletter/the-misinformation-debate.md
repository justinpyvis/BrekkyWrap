  Good morning! Seventy-eight percent of the 1,500 Australian Medical
  Association WA doctors surveyed last week [agreed with the
  claim](https://thewest.com.au/news/coronavirus/australian-medical-association-wa-covid-survey-reveals-most-doctors-dont-think-health-system-is-prepared-c-5481429)
  that "the WA health system was not ready to deal with the inevitable Omicron
  outbreak if we had opened on 5 February".


  Fixing a health system is not done overnight – it's a process that had to be implemented at various points over the past seven years of this government.


  But despite the admission from the state's doctors that the health system isn't ready, over half still believed "the Premier should have stuck to his plan to restart quarantine-free travel on Saturday".


  We're now in a situation where the longer Premier McGowan waits to reopen the Hermit Kingdom, the higher the odds of a disastrous dual Omicron/influenza peak that could quickly overwhelm an already stressed health system.

> "Censors have a fantasy that if they get rid of all the Berensons and Mercolas and Malones, and rein in people like Joe Rogan, that all the holdouts will suddenly rush to get vaccinated. The opposite is true. If you wipe out critics, people will immediately default to higher levels of suspicion. They will now be *sure* there's something wrong with the vaccine. If you want to convince audiences, you have to allow everyone to talk, even the ones you disagree with. You have to make a better case."

So wrote [Matt Taibbi](https://taibbi.substack.com/p/the-folly-of-pandemic-censorship) in response to a growing number of artists – most prominently Neil Young and Joni Mitchell – withdrawing their music from Spotify in response to what they believe is a deliberate attempt to spread misinformation by Spotify's star podcaster, Joe Rogan.

We tend to agree: when has censorship ever worked? Cancelling Joe Rogan – who, incidentally, draws [many more](https://twitter.com/APompliano/status/1478059547688374281) viewers per episode than the competition – will only drive the 'craziness' deeper into the endless abyss that is the internet, where it will fester completely unchecked.

Spotify's solution was to remove Young and Mitchell from the platform, as they requested. It also put a "content advisory" on any podcast episode mentioning COVID-19, and the CEO released a [statement](https://newsroom.spotify.com/2022-01-30/spotifys-platform-rules-and-approach-to-covid-19/) saying "it is important to me that we don't take on the position of being content censor while also making sure that there are rules in place and consequences for those who violate them".

It all comes down to trust. The creators of independent newsletter platform Substack felt the need to emphasise that point in light of the controversy, [writing](https://on.substack.com/p/society-has-a-trust-problem-more):

> "This position has some uncomfortable consequences. It means we allow writers to publish what they want and readers to decide for themselves what to read, even when that content is wrong or offensive, and even when it means putting up with the presence of writers with whom we strongly disagree. But we believe this approach is a necessary precondition for building trust in the information ecosystem as a whole. The more that powerful institutions attempt to control what can and cannot be said in public, the more people there will be who are ready to create alternative narratives about what's "true," spurred by a belief that there’s a conspiracy to suppress important information. When you look at the data, it is clear that these effects are already in full force in society."

Certain politicians and public health officials would do well to heed that advice. Present the costs and benefits, tell the truth and perhaps there would be fewer people marching in the streets every weekend (or as happened yesterday, [Monday](https://www.watoday.com.au/politics/federal/protesters-gather-at-front-entrance-of-parliament-house-20220131-p59smz.html)).
