Guinea's military junta government [signed a deal](https://www.scmp.com/news/world/africa/article/3172072/guineas-junta-signs-us15-billion-deal-vast-simandou-iron-ore-site) yesterday with Australia's Rio Tinto and a consortium to develop the giant Simandou iron ore project, dubbed the "caviar of iron ore" due to its high grade (>65% Fe), with first production expected by 2025.

The agreement comes [just two weeks](https://www.mining.com/guinea-junta-halts-rio-tintos-simandou-iron-ore-project/) after that same government "ordered the cessation of all activity on the ground pending the answers to questions posed to various actors and the clarification of the operational mode by which the interests of Guinea will be preserved".

The project has been touted for decades (Rio Tinto was granted its first exploration licence for the area [in 1997](https://www.mining.com/web/timeline-the-battle-for-simandou/)) but never got off the ground due to the enormous capital costs ‚Äì Guinea's government will not permit the use of the existing rail line and port in Libera (see image above) ‚Äì and near-constant political instability.

Assuming Guinea remains politically stable ‚Äì a big assumption ‚Äì a fully functional Simandou certainly poses problems for Australia, given that it will be capable of shipping up to 100-200 million tonnes of high-grade iron ore each year into a seaborne market currently dominated by Australia (>800 million tonnes) and Brazil (>300 million tonnes).

Iron ore mining companies make up a large part of the Australian share market ‚Äì BHP, FMG and RIO are all in the top 10 by market capitalisation (RIO drifts in and out due to its dual listed status) ‚Äì and iron ore exports generate hundreds of billions of dollars for all levels of Australian governments each year (via company and income tax, royalties and payroll tax, and GST).

An operational Simandou won't kill the Australian iron ore miners or those revenue streams, given its cost of producing and shipping a tonne of iron ore to China will be [at least double](https://asia.nikkei.com/Spotlight/Caixin/China-s-opportunities-and-risks-in-Africa-s-giant-iron-ore-field) what it is from Australia. But it would certainly have a negative effect on price, assuming there's no commensurate increase in demand to offset the new supply.
