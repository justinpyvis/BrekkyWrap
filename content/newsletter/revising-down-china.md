The Lowy Institute published a [report](https://www.lowyinstitute.org/publications/revising-down-rise-china) last week looking at the long-term decline in China's growth, which it expects to "slow to about 3% by 2030 and 2% by 2040, while averaging 2‚Äì3% overall from now until 2050".

It's a long report, so do [check it out](https://www.lowyinstitute.org/publications/revising-down-rise-china). But essentially it warns that given the Chinese government's track record at executing key, productivity-boosting economic reforms (it hasn't), the investment-driven 'catch-up' model it has relied on for so long is approaching its use by date.

Demographics play a large role in the decision to "substantially revise down" China's potential growth (see the chart above). China is well behind where other countries using the 'catch-up' model were given its age profile. While the authors used Korea and Taiwan as examples, we also looked at Japan. 

In terms of GDP per capita (a proxy for wealth), China in 2020 was about where [Japan was in 1983](https://data.worldbank.org/indicator/NY.GDP.PCAP.CD?locations=CN-JP). But Japan's dependency ratio ‚Äì the ratio of aged under 15 or over 64 to the working-age population ‚Äì didn't bottom out [until 1992](https://data.worldbank.org/indicator/SP.POP.DPND?locations=CN-JP), with GDP per capita 200% higher than China's in 2020.

That would all be well and good if China was still in the early days of its demographic dividend. But it's not ‚Äì China's dependency ratio bottomed out in 2010, and the government's "ability to materially offset this demographic outlook is limited".

The authors conclude:

> "China would still likely become the world‚Äôs largest economy. But it would never establish a meaningful lead over the United States and would remain far less prosperous and productive per person than America, even by mid-century."
