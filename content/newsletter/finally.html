Good morning! Australia's Health Secretary, Brendan Murphy, yesterday poured cold water on ScoMo's vaccine hopes, revealing that the 20 million Pfizer doses scheduled to be delivered by the end of this year are "subject to ongoing reassessment with Pfizer".

Australia – one of the last countries to place an order and with no community coronavirus spread – will likely be near the bottom of Pfizer's pecking order.

But never fear, perhaps 2022 will be the year! Victoria yesterday decided to step up to the plate and allocate some cash to kickstart domestic mRNA vaccine production in Australia – but more on that in the Corona Wrap below.

Corona Wrap

Finally!

The Victorian government yesterday announced it will invest $A50 million to "kickstart" domestic manufacturing of mRNA vaccine technology in Australia, the first such facility in the southern hemisphere. However, it will take at least 12 months for any vaccines to be made in Australia.

Stepping back: mRNA is the future of biomedicine. The two best coronavirus vaccines, Pfizer and Moderna, were developed within days using mRNA. According to a 2018 paper in Nature, the technology has also elicited "potent immunity against infectious disease targets in animal models of influenza virus, Zika virus, rabies virus and others", and "promising results" in cancer treatment.

Why it matters: mRNA vaccines are safe, can be 'tweaked' rapidly and are relatively low-cost to manufacture. Once Australia eventually receives its Pfizer vaccine doses from overseas, it won't be long before booster shots are required to deal with coronavirus variants – having domestic productive capacity means when that happens, we won't have to twiddle our thumbs at the back of the queue again. Australia will also be better placed to support its less wealthy neighbours such as Papua New Guinea with this and any future health crises.
