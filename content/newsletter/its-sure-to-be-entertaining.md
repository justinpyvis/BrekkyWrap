intro: >-
  Good morning! According to the National Bureau of Statistics, China's economy
  [expanded by
  4.8%](https://www.cnbc.com/2022/04/18/china-economy-q1-gdp-beats-expectations-to-grow-4point8percent-yoy.html)
  in the March quarter from a year ago, beating expectations "despite the impact
  of Covid lockdowns in parts of the country in March".


  In terms of the most recent monthly data, retail sales tanked -3.5% from a year earlier in March (hard to do much shopping when locked down!) but fixed asset investment (+9.3%) and industrial production (+5.0%) outperformed. 


  The longer China's government persists with its "dynamic zero-COVID strategy", the greater the divergence between consumers and industry will become. Any ambitions Xi Jinping might have had at 'rebalancing' away from low-productivity investment growth this year (e.g. infrastructure) will have to be tossed out the window again to stave off a recession, at the cost of a more difficult adjustment and lower growth in the future.


  How times have changed. To think that only a year ago publications such as the NYT were singing the praises of China's "authoritarian approach" to the pandemic...

  The **US S&P500** edged lower last night, closing down -0.02% with no major
  data releases or overseas markets to provide guidance. A positive [earnings
  report](https://www.cnbc.com/2022/04/18/bac-earnings-1q-2022.html) from Bank

  jumped on the recession bandwagon by warning that:


  > "...the policy tightening we expect raises the odds of recession. As a result, we now see the odds of a recession as roughly 15% in the next 12 months and 35% within the next 24 months."


  The euro hit a 2-year low against the US dollar after the European Central Bank – where inflation was running at 7.5% annually [in March](https://www.politico.eu/article/eurozone-inflation-soars-to-record-7-5-percent-in-march/), more than triple its target – left rates unchanged at its latest meeting, instead [warning of](https://money.usnews.com/investing/news/articles/2022-04-13/fearing-high-inflation-ecb-to-stay-on-course-to-unwind-stimulus) "downside risks to the growth outlook".

The social media chatter over the long weekend was all about Elon Musk (what a surprise!), after he [made an offer](https://www.cnbc.com/2022/04/14/elon-musk-offers-to-buy-twitter-for-54point20-a-share-saying-it-needs-to-be-transformed-as-private-company.html) to buy social media platform Twitter for $US54.20 a share, a 38% premium to the share price on 1 April and a valuation of $US43.4 billion.

Musk currently owns 9.2% of Twitter and last week declined an offer to join the board, which would have prevented him from acquiring more than 14.9% of the company.

Twitter's board, [which contains](https://twitter.com/ChrisJBakke/status/1515377028295389186) "a couple PhDs, a few MBAs, and a Baroness who use Twitter once a year (to reset their passwords) and collectively own 77 shares of the company", [quickly implemented](https://techcrunch.com/2022/04/15/twitter-adopts-poison-pill-defense-to-block-elon-musk-takeover/) a so-called "poison pill":

> "For a limited time, existing Twitter shareholders will be able to purchase additional shares at a discount under certain circumstances. If an entity, person or group crosses a threshold, in this case a 15% stake in Twitter, other shareholders can buy more shares. This way, it would stagger plans to buy more than 15% of the company."

In terms of *why* Musk wants to buy Twitter, there are two likely reasons. One is Musk's description of himself as a "[free speech absolutist](https://twitter.com/elonmusk/status/1499976967105433600)", who has become increasingly frustrated with censorship on social media which [he says](https://twitter.com/elonmusk/status/1507259709224632344) "is essential to a functioning democracy". 

The other reason is that Twitter has been a [relatively](https://twitter.com/jack/status/1515549577352564740) [poorly](https://twitter.com/benedictevans/status/1514555699208065034) managed company by tech industry standards, perhaps best explained by the fact its management has almost no skin in the game (the [principal-agent problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal%E2%80%93agent_problem)). By taking Twitter private Musk could cull the board, recalibrate the interests of the owners and management and eventually turn a buck on the deal.

How Musk proceeds is now the great uncertainty. He's temperamental enough to do anything from partnering with the likes of [Peter Thiel](https://twitter.com/mcuban/status/1514617987206066182) to get around the poison pill, to simply flogging his shares and walking away. Whatever happens, it's sure to be entertaining! 🍿
